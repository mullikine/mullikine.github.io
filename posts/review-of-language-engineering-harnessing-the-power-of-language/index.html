Review of 'Language Engineering; Harnessing the Power of Language (2004)'

Dec 17, 2019
3 min read
LE

This article is from 2004 or earlier.

It’s still an interesting insight into perspectives on Language Engineering from the early 2000s.

Original article
Language Engineering; Harnessing the Power of Language

The use of language is currently restricted.

Even between humans, understanding is usually limited to those groups who share a common language. †

†
…let alone the language barrier with computers.

Language can be seen as much a barrier to communication as an aid.

A change is taking place which will revolutionise our use of language and greatly enhance the value of language in every aspect of communication.

This change is the result of developments in Language Engineering.

Available resources

  • Electronic dictionaries
  • grammars
  • terminology banks
  • corpora

Represent the KB needed to:

  • recognise,
  • validate,
  • understand, and
  • manipulate language

using the power of computers.

Language is Fundamental

Language is:

  • A means of effective, efficient communication.
  • A medium for recording and assimilating information conveniently.
  • A personal identity / fingerprint.
My thoughts
The article referred to cultural identity, but I the notion should be taken further. A person has their own language and that is what we should be targetting.

Definition of Language Engineering circa. 2004

Language Engineering
The application of knowledge of language to the development of computer systems which can recognise, understand, interpret, and generate human language in all its forms.

In practice, Language Engineering comprises:

  • A set of techniques
    Implemented in computer software.
  • A set of language resources
    A repository of knowledge which can be accessed by computer software.
My thoughts
Modern Language Engineering would extend well beyond human languages.

I skipped a bunch of the article here

It’s discussing NLP subtopics.

Grammars

A grammar describes the structure of a language at different levels:

  • word (morphological grammar),
  • phrase,
  • sentence, etc.

A grammar can deal with structure both in terms of:

  • surface (syntax)
  • meaning (semantics and discourse).

Corpora

A corpus is a body of language, either text or speech, which provides the basis for:

  • Analysis of language to establish its characteristics.
  • Training a machine, usually to adapt its behaviour to particular circumstances.
  • Verifying empirically a theory concerning language.
  • A test set for a Language Engineering technique or application to establish how well it works in practice.

Model of Language Engineering Activities

Language Engineering is applied at two levels.

first level

Generic classes of application:

  • language translation
  • information management (multi-lingual)
  • authoring (multi-lingual)
  • human/machine interface (multi-lingual voice and text)

second level

The enabling applications are applied to real world problems across the social and economic spectrum.

  • human language translation
    Currently used to provide translator workbenches and automatic translation in limited domains.

    • My thoughts: This has to be extended to animal, plant, robot and alien languages.

  • information management
    Can be used in an information service, as the basis for analysing requests for information and matching the request, against a database of text or images, to select the information accurately.

  • authoring tools
    Typically used in word processing systems but can also be used to generate text, such as business letters in foreign languages, as well as in conjunction with information management, to provide document management facilities.

  • user interfaces
    Most applications can usefully be provided with natural language user interfaces, including speech, to improve their usability.

    • My thoughts: Great emphasis seems to be placed on this. I think by now this concept should extend into interfacing/merging natural languages with programming languages.
